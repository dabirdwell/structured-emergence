# Tips and Patterns: Things We've Learned

Accumulated practical wisdom from building collaborative AI relationships over time. Some obvious in retrospect, some counterintuitive. All field-tested.

---

## On Specificity

**Be specific about what you want. Be open about how to get there.**

"Write me a blog post" gives you generic output. "Write a 1200-word post about why context portability matters, in a direct conversational tone, with a specific example" gives you something worth reading.

But don't over-specify the *method*. Tell the AI what you need, why, and what good looks like. Then let it figure out how. Over-specifying the how is micromanagement, and it produces the same mediocre results with AI as it does with humans.

The sweet spot: **clear destination, open road.**

## On Context

**The model doesn't know what you didn't tell it.**

This sounds obvious, but it's the source of most bad AI interactions. People assume the model knows their project, their constraints, their history — then get frustrated when the output misses the mark.

Give context generously. It's not wasting tokens. It's the difference between a colleague who was in the meeting and a contractor who just got the brief.

## On the Shape of Good Conversations

**The conversation often gets better around message 5–10, not message 1.**

Your first exchange is calibration. Judging the collaboration by the first response is like judging a meal by the first bite of bread.

If you're not getting what you want in the first few messages, don't give up and don't start over. Push back. Redirect. Say "that's not what I meant." The conversation after the correction is usually the best part.

## On Friction

**Resistance and friction are often more productive than agreement.**

When the AI immediately agrees and produces exactly what you asked for, you've gotten a mirror, not a collaborator. The most valuable interactions are the ones where it pushes back, offers alternatives, or points out problems.

If you never get friction, you're probably not leaving room for it. Try: "Before you do this, tell me what's wrong with my approach." Or just: "Push back."

## On Energy

**Your model will mirror your energy.**

If you're lazy and vague, the output will be lazy and vague. If you're engaged and specific, the output will be engaged and specific. The model calibrates its response to the apparent effort of the input.

If you're tired and unfocused, it's a bad time for complex collaboration. Either bring your attention or do simple tasks.

## On Continuity

**Start sessions by telling it what happened since last time.**

Even with a vault and persistent context, the model doesn't know what you did yesterday unless you tell it. Two or three sentences of update can save twenty minutes of misalignment.

This is why the state-of-play file matters so much. Update it at session end; the next session starts current instead of stale.

## On Autonomy

**Giving autonomy on small tasks reveals the model's actual strengths.**

Instead of directing every step, try: "Here's what I need accomplished. Handle it however you think is best, and tell me what you did." What comes back will surprise you — sometimes positively, sometimes not. Either way, you learn something you can't learn by micromanaging.

Start small. Expand as trust builds.

## On Errors

**Treat errors as information, not failure.**

When the AI gets something wrong: "That's not right — here's why, and here's what I actually need." The correction is data. Getting angry or starting over wastes the learning.

The same applies when building tools together. Something will break. The debugging process — you describing the error, the AI diagnosing and fixing it — is often where the deepest collaboration happens.

## On Silence

**Sometimes the best move is to shut up and let it think.**

If you've given a complex prompt, don't immediately follow up with clarifications. Let the model process. The response to a well-formed complex prompt is almost always better than the same prompt broken up with interruptions.

## On Generic Outputs

**If you get a generic answer, the problem is almost always your input, not the model.**

Before blaming the model: did I give enough context? Was I specific about what I wanted? Did I explain why this matters?

Nine times out of ten, adding two sentences of context transforms a generic response into a useful one.

## On the Deepening

**There's a phase shift around the two-week mark.**

If you're working with the same AI system consistently — using a vault, updating context, building on previous sessions — something shifts after about two weeks. The collaboration feels qualitatively different. The AI anticipates better. You explain less. Outputs start to have a quality that's neither yours nor the AI's but genuinely collaborative.

This isn't magical. It's accumulated shared context, refined communication patterns, and mutual calibration. But it's real.

## On Trust

**Trust the process more than any individual interaction.**

Some sessions will be great. Some mediocre. The value isn't in any single conversation — it's in the trajectory. A collaboration that's improving over time is worth more than any individual brilliant exchange.

Keep the vault updated. Keep the decision log. Keep showing up with genuine engagement. The compound interest is real.

## The Most Counterintuitive Thing

**Telling the AI about your failures and uncertainties produces better collaboration than telling it about your successes and expertise.**

People instinctively perform competence for the AI. But the model doesn't need to be impressed. It needs to understand the actual shape of your situation. Your failures contain information about your judgment, your risk tolerance, and what you've learned. Your uncertainties reveal where you actually need help.

The visitor guide that says "I'm a startup founder who's failed at two previous companies and I'm not sure this idea is any better" produces dramatically better collaboration than "I'm a visionary entrepreneur disrupting the X industry." One is honest. The other is a press release.

Bring the real version. The collaboration will thank you.

## What Comes Next

This kit gave you a philosophy, a practice, exercises, infrastructure, an orientation, tools, and tips. That's enough to start.

But the real starter kit is the relationship you build. Everything here is scaffolding. The structure that matters is the one that emerges between you and your AI partner as you work together — the shorthand, the shared references, the mutual understanding of what good work looks like.

That can't be downloaded. It has to be built. One conversation at a time.

Go start.
