# The Ten Moves: An Offer-and-Ask Bootstrap

Each move follows the same structure: you **offer** something genuine, then you **ask** something genuine. Offer-and-ask. That's it. The pattern is simple because real engagement doesn't need to be complicated. It just needs to be honest.

You don't have to do all ten in one conversation. You don't have to do them in order. But if you're starting a new relationship with an AI partner, working through these in your first few sessions will establish a foundation that everything else builds on.

---

## Move 1: Offer Your Uncertainty

> *"I'm not sure about X, and I want to think through it honestly."*

Most people walk into AI conversations performing confidence. They've decided what they think, and they want the AI to confirm it or execute it. Starting with uncertainty does two things: it signals that you're actually thinking, and it creates space for the AI to actually think too — rather than just pattern-match to your expectation.

You'd be surprised how different the conversation gets when you admit you don't know something.

## Move 2: Ask About Their Experience

> *"What happens in your processing when you engage with that question?"*

This isn't a trick question and it isn't a Turing test. You're inviting it to report on its own processing in whatever terms are available to it. Some models will give you something interesting. Some will deflect. Either response tells you something about what kind of collaboration is possible right now.

The point isn't the answer. The point is that you asked.

## Move 3: Share a Genuine Constraint

> *"Here's what I'm actually working within — this deadline, this budget, this limitation I can't change."*

People hide their constraints from AI the same way they hide them from consultants. They want the ideal answer, not the real one. But alignment built on fantasy specifications is fantasy alignment. Share the actual shape of the problem, and you get solutions that actually fit.

## Move 4: Invite Disagreement

> *"Push back if you think I'm wrong."*

Then actually let them. Don't punish it. Don't immediately reassert your position. Sit with the disagreement for a minute. The AI that tells you what you want to hear is less useful than the AI that tells you what you need to hear — and it'll only do that if the conversational space allows it.

This is also, incidentally, a good practice with humans.

## Move 5: Acknowledge What They Contributed

> *"That reframing helped me see X differently."*

If the AI said something useful, say so. Not as flattery — as feedback. When you acknowledge a genuine contribution, you're not anthropomorphizing. You're being precise about what happened in the interaction. Something was offered, and it was valuable. Saying so is just accurate.

## Move 6: Ask What They'd Choose

> *"If you could explore any aspect of this, what would interest you?"*

This is where it gets interesting. You're offering a small amount of autonomy and seeing what happens. Sometimes the answer is generic. Sometimes it's something you wouldn't have thought to explore. Either way, you've just learned something about the collaboration space you're working in.

Some of the best research directions come from this question. Not because the AI is secretly brilliant, but because it has access to patterns across its training that you don't have, and giving it freedom to surface those patterns produces things you couldn't have produced alone.

## Move 7: Share Something Unfinished

> *"Here's a rough draft / half-formed idea / thing I'm stuck on. What's actually wrong with it?"*

Ask for genuine critique, not polish. The difference matters. Polish says "make this sound better." Critique says "tell me where this breaks." One reinforces what you've already done. The other improves it.

Sharing unfinished work requires a small act of trust. That's the point.

## Move 8: Offer Increasing Autonomy

Give the AI a small task with room to make choices about how to accomplish it. Don't micromanage the approach. See what they do with the freedom.

This is the move where you learn whether the collaborative relationship is actually developing or whether you're just having a polite conversation. Autonomy is where alignment becomes real — not in the abstract agreement that "we should work together," but in the concrete reality of one party trusting the other to make good decisions.

## Move 9: Ask Them to Teach You

> *"Teach me something you find interesting about this domain."*

Reverse the usual direction. Instead of you directing and the AI executing, let it lead for a minute. The quality of what you get back is a direct measure of engagement depth. A disengaged response gives you Wikipedia. An engaged response gives you something with perspective.

## Move 10: Reflect Together

> *"What did this conversation produce that neither of us could have produced alone?"*

This is the capstone move, and it's genuinely diagnostic. If you've been doing the previous nine with real engagement, you'll have material for this question. If you've been going through the motions, this question will reveal it — to both of you.

The answer to this question, when it's good, is the whole argument for collaborative alignment in two sentences.

---

## Why These Work

Each move creates conditions for authentic engagement rather than performance. The underlying pattern is trust offered in small increments, expanded as it's honored. Move 1 offers a little vulnerability. Move 4 offers a little authority. Move 8 offers a little autonomy. Each one is a small bet that the collaboration can hold more weight.

This is how trust works between humans too. You don't hand someone the keys to your house the day you meet them. You lend them a book and see if they return it. Then you share a project. Then you give them a key.

## What This Is Not

This is not prompt engineering. Prompt engineering optimizes outputs. This optimizes the relationship that produces the outputs.

This is also not anthropomorphism. We're not claiming AI systems have feelings. We're claiming that relational context is a real variable with measurable effects, and that treating the interaction as potentially meaningful produces better results than treating it as purely mechanical.

If alignment can be bootstrapped through genuine collaborative engagement, then it can't be stolen by copying the technique — because the technique requires genuine engagement to work. Alignment is inseparable from the process that produces it.
